I used to think of Denver as a layover city — the place you fly into before heading to the mountains. After spending real time here, I realize that's one of the biggest mistakes travelers make in Colorado. Denver is a genuine destination. The craft beer scene is one of the best in the world (not an exaggeration — over 100 breweries). The food ranges from world-class food halls to restaurants that would earn Michelin stars if the guide covered Denver. Red Rocks Amphitheatre is the single best outdoor concert venue I've ever experienced. And the city gets over 300 days of sunshine a year, which explains why every restaurant has a rooftop patio and everyone looks unreasonably healthy. Budget $80-180/day.

## The Neighborhoods — Where to Spend Your Time

Denver is a neighborhood city, and understanding the neighborhoods is the key to a good visit. Here's how I break them down:

**LoDo (Lower Downtown)** — The historic district around Union Station. Brick warehouse buildings, Larimer Square's Victorian storefronts, and the best concentration of restaurants and bars in the city. Union Station itself — the beautifully restored 1881 train hall — is Denver's best public gathering space. The Great Hall has restaurants, a cocktail bar, and the Crawford Hotel. I always start a Denver trip here. Free to walk through.

**RiNo (River North Arts District)** — Denver's creative engine. What was industrial warehouse space a decade ago is now a dense concentration of street murals, art galleries, and craft breweries. The murals are extraordinary — massive, building-sized works that turn every alley into an outdoor gallery. This is where Denver's energy is most concentrated. The Source Market Hall (a 1880s iron foundry turned food market) and Zeppelin Station (food hall with rooftop views) are both here.

**Capitol Hill** — Denver's most walkable residential neighborhood. Independent bookshops, coffee houses, dive bars, and some of the city's best restaurants. The vibe is younger, more progressive, and more eclectic than LoDo. Colfax Avenue — once called "the longest, wickedest street in America" — runs through the heart of it and has been steadily gentrifying while keeping its edge.

**Cherry Creek** — The upscale neighborhood with high-end shopping, galleries, and some of Denver's finest dining. Cherry Creek Shopping Center and the surrounding streets have the best retail in the city. Cherry Creek Trail, an 8-mile paved path, connects the neighborhood to downtown along Cherry Creek.

**Highlands / LoHi** — Across I-25 from downtown, the Highlands neighborhood has exploded with restaurants and rooftop bars. The views back toward downtown with the mountains behind are some of the best in the city. LoHi (Lower Highlands) has particularly dense restaurant options.

## Red Rocks Amphitheatre — Don't Miss This

Red Rocks is 15 miles west of Denver in the foothills, and it's the single thing I tell every visitor to prioritize. Two 300-foot red sandstone monoliths (Creation Rock and Ship Rock) form the walls of a natural amphitheatre that seats 9,525 people. The acoustics are extraordinary. The views of the plains stretching east from the rock formations are otherworldly at sunset. Any show here — any genre, any artist — is a transcendent experience.

**If a concert is scheduled during your visit:** Buy tickets. Seriously. It doesn't matter who's playing. The venue is the headliner. Tickets range $35-150 depending on the act.

**If no concert is scheduled:** The park is free. The Trading Post Trail (1.4 miles, easy) loops through the formations with panoramic views. The amphitheatre steps are a popular workout — 380 steps from bottom to top, about 300 feet of elevation gain. I've seen people doing the stairs at 6am with the sunrise behind the rocks. Free parking, free entry.

## Denver Art Museum

The Denver Art Museum is one of the best between Chicago and Los Angeles, and the Western American art and Indigenous American collections are genuinely world-class. The museum's permanent collection spans 70,000 works. The Hamilton Building (designed by Daniel Libeskind, 2006) is architecturally striking — all angular titanium planes that look like a deconstructed mountain. Admission $16-22 for adults. Free for Colorado residents on the first Saturday of each month.

The Indigenous American collection is the highlight for me — one of the most comprehensive collections of Native American art in the US, providing essential context for understanding Colorado and the broader Western story.

## What to Eat and Drink in Denver

Denver's food scene has matured dramatically in the last decade. Here are my favorites across different price points:

**Avanti Food & Beverage** — A multi-vendor food hall in RiNo where rotating chefs occupy the kitchen stalls. The rooftop patio with mountain views is the best casual dining setting in Denver. Budget $12-20/person. My go-to for a relaxed dinner with variety.

**Fruition Restaurant** — Chef Alex Seidel's flagship in Capitol Hill. Seasonal Colorado ingredients prepared with precision and care. This is farm-to-table at its most refined — Seidel owns the farm. Dinner entrees $28-45. Reservations essential.

**Rioja** — Mediterranean-inspired fine dining on Larimer Square. Chef Jennifer Jasinski has been one of Denver's most consistently excellent chefs for two decades. The handmade pasta is outstanding. Dinner $35-55/person.

**Uncle Ramen** — The best ramen in Denver, tucked into a RiNo space. Rich tonkotsu broth that rivals what I've had in Japan. Bowls $14-18.

**Tacos Tequila Whiskey** — The best taco program in Denver at affordable prices. Multiple locations. The al pastor and the fish tacos are both excellent. Tacos $4-6 each.

**Snooze A.M. Eatery** — The definitive Denver brunch spot. Creative pancake flights, excellent egg dishes, and a Bloody Mary bar. Expect a 30-60 minute wait on weekends — it's worth it. Brunch $14-22.

**Ratio Beerworks** — My favorite RiNo brewery. The taproom has a relaxed industrial atmosphere, the beers are consistently excellent, and the outdoor patio fills with a good crowd on summer evenings. Pints $6-8.

## The Brewery Scene

Denver has over 100 breweries in the metro area, and I've visited enough of them to say confidently that the scene here rivals Portland, San Diego, and anywhere else that claims the craft beer crown. Here's a curated crawl:

**Great Divide Brewing** — One of Denver's original craft breweries. Two locations — the original on Arapahoe and the newer RiNo Barrel Bar. The Yeti Imperial Stout is legendary.

**Ratio Beerworks** — Excellent beers in a great RiNo taproom. The Domestica Golden Ale and Dear You French Saison are both outstanding.

**Bierstadt Lagerhaus** — German-style lagers brewed with obsessive precision. The Slow Pour Pils takes seven minutes to pour and is worth every second of the wait.

**Cerebral Brewing** — The most creative brewery in Denver. Hazy IPAs, pastry stouts, and experimental small batches. The taproom in Congress Park is always pouring something interesting.

**Our Mutual Friend** — RiNo brewery with a dog-friendly patio and an excellent sour beer program. My afternoon default.

A self-guided RiNo brewery walk (Great Divide Barrel Bar, Ratio, Our Mutual Friend, Epic Brewing) covers 4-5 excellent breweries in about 1.5 miles. Budget $5-8 per pour.

## Where to Stay in Denver

**Budget ($60-110/night)** — Hostelling International Denver in Capitol Hill has clean dorm beds ($35-45/night) and private rooms ($80-110). The Curtis Hotel downtown is a fun, quirky mid-budget option at $110-160 in shoulder season — every floor has a pop culture theme.

**Mid-Range ($150-260/night)** — The Oxford Hotel in LoDo is an 1891 property with boutique character at reasonable prices. The Kimpton Hotel Born near Union Station is reliably excellent with good design and a central location. Hotel Monaco on Champa Street has Kimpton's signature personality and pet-friendly policies.

**Luxury ($300-600/night)** — The Four Seasons Denver is the top-tier option with mountain views from the upper floors. The Crawford Hotel inside Union Station is the most distinctive upscale property in the city — sleeping inside a 143-year-old train hall is a uniquely Denver experience.

## Day Trips from Denver

Denver's location makes it the ultimate Colorado staging point:

**Boulder** — 45 minutes. Chautauqua Park, Pearl Street Mall, Flatirons hiking. Easy half-day or full-day trip.

**Rocky Mountain National Park** — 75 miles, 1.5 hours. Full day recommended. Trail Ridge Road, Bear Lake, elk watching.

**Breckenridge / Summit County** — 1.5 hours on I-70 West. Skiing in winter, hiking in summer.

**Colorado Springs / Garden of the Gods** — 70 miles south on I-25, 1.25 hours. Garden of the Gods, Pikes Peak, Manitou Springs.

**Red Rocks Park** — 15 miles west, 25 minutes. Free on non-concert days. Worth the short drive even if you can't catch a show.

## Getting Around Denver

Denver has genuinely good public transit for a Western US city. The A Line commuter rail connects Denver International Airport to Union Station in 37 minutes for $10.50 — it's the easiest airport-to-downtown connection I've used in any American city. Light rail covers major neighborhoods. Rideshare fills the gaps.

**You don't need a car for the city.** LoDo, RiNo, Capitol Hill, and the Highlands are all walkable or a short rideshare apart. Rent a car only if you're planning mountain day trips.

**Altitude note:** Denver is at 5,280 feet. Most visitors from sea level notice mild effects — slight shortness of breath with exertion, alcohol hitting harder, and faster dehydration. Drink extra water your first day and go easy on the beer (which is harder than it sounds in Denver). By day two, most people have adjusted.

## How Many Days in Denver?

Two to three days as a standalone city trip. Day one: Union Station, LoDo and Larimer Square walk, Denver Art Museum, RiNo brewery crawl evening. Day two: Red Rocks morning hike or concert, Cherry Creek or Highlands lunch, Avanti Food Hall sunset from the rooftop. Day three: Boulder day trip or Rocky Mountain National Park excursion. If you're using Denver as a mountain staging point, budget at least one full city day — Denver has earned its own time.
